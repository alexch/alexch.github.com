The filibuster is not in the Constitution; it is a Senate rule of procedure that was itself enacted with a simple majority vote. Perhaps if more people were aware of what it really was and in what authority it is grounded, they would be more amenable to changing it...

Personally, I favor giving Senators who are extremely devoted to a cause the ability to call attention to it and hold up a vote by speaking publicly at length. But this business of requiring a cloture vote to move forward, rather than a cloture vote to shut up the person holding the floor, makes it too easy for anyone to obstruct anything and puts the burden on the majority rather than the minority, where it should be. I followed Wikipedia's statement, "According to the Supreme Court ruling in United States v. Ballin (1892), changes to Senate rules could be achieved by a simple majority. Nevertheless, under current Senate rules, a rule change itself could be filibustered, and in this case votes from three fifths of Senators would be required to break the filibuster." http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filibuster_in_the_U.S._Senate


The citation on that statement linked to http://rules.senate.gov/public/index.cfm?p=RuleXXII

I guessing that this paragraph is the important one:
"'Is it the sense of the Senate that the debate shall be brought to a close?' And if that question shall be decided in the affirmative by three-fifths of the Senators duly chosen and sworn -- except on a measure or motion to amend the Senate rules, in which case the necessary affirmative vote shall be two-thirds of the Senators present and voting -- then said measure, motion, or other matter pending before the Senate, or the unfinished business, shall be the unfinished business to the exclusion of all other business until disposed of."

There's a chance that wikipedia is wrong and that I've misread that paragraph. I'm not an expert in the rules of US Senate. Oct 29, 2010 Oct 29, 2010
