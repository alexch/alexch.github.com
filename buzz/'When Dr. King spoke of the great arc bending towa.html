Michael Sofaer 115596999507185327673
People in aggregate are moved by systems, they have not become less wise on average in the last 15 years. What actually happened is that corporations engineered sales processes and products that are more successful on average in convincing people to undertake actions not in their interest. Blaming the masses for the mortgage crisis is like blaming your site's slowness on the web requests taking longer.

When something changes on the scale of how the mortgage market changed, it's not because people changed, it's because the system changed. There's room for discussion around what the main problem was, personally I'm going with the rating agencies' discovery that selling ratings for money is better business than honestly rating instruments, but something systemic caused people to behave differently. Kevin Christmas 106500313906019086377
@Michael It's not that simple. Sure, the ratings agencies didn't do their job, but that's only part of it. People who couldn't afford what they were buying let themselves get talked into buying something they couldn't afford on the promise that the value would go up and they could refinance before the ARM reset. The agencies underwriting the housing loans didn't do their job. They underwrote loans to people who had fraudulent applications or weren't qualified for a loan. The agencies purchasing the loans from the shady mortgage brokers didn't do their jobs. The funds purchasing the loans to hold over a long period of time didn't do their job.

Through out the whole securitization process of mortgages, incentives were in place to not look closely at the underlying borrowers. Due diligence costs money and the loans were only going to be held long enough to sell them.

If just one of those agencies along the process did their job, we probably would have avoided this whole mess. Aug 09, 2011 Aug 09, 2011
