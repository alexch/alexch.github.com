I know there's a lot of research that shows that it's counterproductive, that much investment actually kicks into gear when the economy is out of recession, that it's unclear that even well-targeted spending isn't also rife with unintended consequences.

It's not that Keynesianism is wrong and something else is right. It's that evidence is all over the place for both Keynesianism and the alternatives...macro is a big mess and humanity barely "knows" anything about macro. My sense is there may on the whole be some good reason to lean toward a Keynesian outlook in times of extreme distress, when nobody has better ideas, like 2008, but I'm just guessing.

When you look at the intellectuals on both sides of the Hayek/Friedman vs Keynes divide, you see people gravitating toward one or the other not on the merits, or on the basis of intellectual honesty or scientific inquiry, etc - rather it's pretty obvious that people are aligning mostly based on political inclinations. Lefties think Keynes just makes sense, for whatever reason (probably comfort with govt solving collective action problems). Libertarians think Hayek just makes sense (extreme distrust of the govt's ability to do anything well).

But you don't see Libertarians standing up and saying, "you know, on the evidence, I was wrong about Keynes". That's because there's no hard evidence because econ is not a hard science.

I came across a good argument a while back (forget where) that sociology is on much more solid footing than econ, in spite of the pinko reputation, because the methods realistically take into account that it's only possible to go so far with actual "science". Whereas econ is blinkered by this advanced math obsession...in econ you're proving, in sociology you're searching. Something like that.

Btw a corollary to this point about macroeconomics is certainty some people have that people like the treasury secretary and fed chairman don't know what they're doing. Certainty about matters like these is revealing. The people who are genuinely certain just aren't aware of how confused their assumptions and premises are (or, they're one of the few like Krugman who at some level are aware but are basically in the entertainment business). The CEA uses the same models to make their predictions as they do to claim their results. It's hardly proof of anything other than that the models yield consistent results.

"First, there are model simulations. That is, the CEA took a conventional Keynesian-style macroeconomic model and used those set of equations to estimate the effect the stimulus should have had. Essentially, the model offers an estimate of the policy's effect, conditional on the model being a correct description of the world. But notice that this exercise is not really a measurement based on what actually occurred. Rather, the exercise is premised on the belief that the model is true, so no matter how bad the economy got, the inference is that it would have been even worse without the stimulus. Why? Because that is what the model says. The validity of the model itself is never questioned."
