Going into default isn't the same as declaring the debt invalid. Banks don't foreclose on a house because the borrower can't pay and declares the mortgage invalid.

Yes, not paying on time is a violation of the agreement, but the agreement is still in place and typically, there are penalties associated.

"So there's this law. The law could be broken. In the case that it's broken, who should enforce the law?"

Civil claims against the government are handled by the court system. The party that was harmed, i.e. the bond holder, has standing. If section 4 of the 14th amendment didn't exist, then Congress could invalidate debts. Any attempts to sue for the invalidated debt would be dismissed. Because section 4 does exist, even if Congress passes a law to invalidate a debt, the debt holders still have standing to sue. Jul 09, 2011 Jul 09, 2011

Laurence H. Tribe, a professor of constitutional law at Harvard, explains, "The Constitution grants only Congress — not the president — the power 'to borrow money on the credit of the United States.' Nothing in the 14th Amendment or in any other constitutional provision suggests that the president may usurp legislative power to prevent a violation of the Constitution. Moreover, it is well established that the president’s power drops to what Justice Robert H. Jackson called its 'lowest ebb' when exercised against the express will of Congress."
