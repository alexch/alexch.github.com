The irony of this blog post is that banks are just like Bill Gates. They don't have any more cash than they are required to have, but they have lots of government bonds.

With lots of shaky mortgages still on the books, it's a no-brainer for the banks. Invest in almost risk free government bonds with the money that you're paying no interest on, or make a risky loan.

In other words, banks are making loans. It's just that they're making them to the government. It's a classic case of the government crowding out investment in the private sector.

Cut government spending, investments in the private sector will rise. Sep 04, 2010 Sep 05, 2010

We were in a liquidity trap in 2008. Banks weren't lending to anyone, including the government. Lehman Brothers failed because investors pulled all their money out. LIBOR had a nice spike in it.

When every dollar spent by the government counts as a dollar in the GDP, you have to do some massaging to come up with those numbers. I am uncomfortable calculating GDP that way. A 300 dollar toilet seats counts as 300 dollars towards the GDP. Sure, the private sector pays more than the market price for things too. Rarely, does that over spending exceed the consumer's value though.

However, my main argument is that government debt is crowding out private debt. It was a response to Matthew Yglesias blog post that was basically advocating that the government do a "helicopter drop." Yglesias was assuming that the banks are just sitting on the cash.
