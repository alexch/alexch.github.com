Um, ok, if you say so. Not only don't I agree with that, I don't see a very complete understanding of selection and adaptation on the author's part. Hamilton, Dawkins and other proponents of kin selection are very clear that individuals don't have to be equipped to do the math when it comes to kin selection (for example, they don't pause before running into a burning building and ask "the person inside likely only shares 1/16th of my genes; is this really worth it?").

This is where Wilson got tripped up as well, because he couldn't resist trying to draw a hard line between kin and non-kin. In reality, evolution only requires a tiny bit of advantage for an adaptation to be kept (and some things are kept long after they're useful, too). So one person or many try to save a dangerous animal or some non-kin and die uselessly. So what? It only matters if the ones that reproduce had the bit where you might try to be altruistic sometimes, and that's good enough. (And kin used to be close by, all the time, for a long time.)

Altruism arises/arose from kin selection, which is a consequence of gene selection. That's enough to provide basis for the adaptation & behavior, and once you have that individuals can use it wherever & whenever they choose, and there's nothing mysterious about it, really. Apr 22, 2011 Apr 22, 2011

Elliotte Rusty Harold 105668412117293290482
I'm not sure kin selection is insufficient to explain pet ownership and tsunami dogs. The mistake a lot of folks make is treating evolution as a proxy for God or design. I.e. they assumes it's maximally efficient. But as Gould pointed out in The Panda's Thumb what distinguishes evolution from theism is precisely the mistakes and the inefficiencies.

If there's an evolutionary advantage to being attracted to small creatures with big eyes that are warm and cuddly, then there's no guarantee that the only such creatures we'll be attracted to are our own babies. Especially when those creatures have 25,000 years of unnatural selection making them more and more like what we're biologically attracted to in each generation. As long as it's reasonably likely that small mammals with big eyes in our immediate vicinity are related to us, it's plausible that kin selection could explain why we care about all small mammals with big eyes in our immediate vicinity. Perhaps this could also explain why we tend to care more about a stray dog right in front of us than a starving child on the other side of the planet. Anything that's right in front of us is more likely to be our kin.

And if there's an evolutionary advantage to distinguishing between small human mammals with big eyes and small canine mammals with big eyes. there's no guarantee at all that evolution will come up with that trait. Evolution leads to imperfect beings, by any reasonable definition of perfect. Apr 22, 2011 Apr 22, 2011
