Elliotte Rusty Harold 105668412117293290482
Is economic growth a nice to have? That depends on how you define growth. A lot of our measures of growth such as GDP are based on outdated, outmoded ways of thinking that have worked less and less well every year for the last 40+ years. As the world moves more and more to an economy based on services and intangible IP, the whole notion of growth based on totaling up all the good in the world gets more out of date every day.

For instance:

1. Is a 30" CRT TV worth more than a 19" flat screen LCD?
2. Is a big, gas guzzling Cadillac worth more than a Prius?
3. Is a 60W incandescent light bulb worth more than a 13W compact fluorescent?
4. Is GM worth more than Google?
5. Is a mined diamond worth more than a higher quality lab created diamond?
6. Is a big Hollywood production worth more than an Indy film cut in the director's hotel room?
7. Is a strip mall worth more than a park?
8. Is a child's long hospital stay worth more than a quick antibiotic shot that clears up the problem?
9. Is an afternoon spent working on an assembly line worth more than an afternoon spent playing with one's children?

By traditional measures such as GDP, these things are generally true. The measures are based on how much economic activity is involved than on any sort of intrinsic value.The compact fluorescent is the most obvious example. It produces the same light as a 60W incandescent, so I'd say it has the same intrinsic value, but replacing incandescents with fluorescents makes a negative contribution to growth over the bulb's lifetime.

Even for the most basic goods such as food, parts of the world are surpassing maximum per capita values. In the United States, for example, there have been concerted, deliberate, and successful efforts by both business and government to increase per capita food consumption beyond any level that is healthy or necessary. Simply put the U.S. food industry has become so efficient that it produces more cheap calories than even an increasing population can or should consume. More growth in the U.S. corn crop, for example, is neither necessary nor helpful. (Yes, I recognize that not all of the world has reached this state yet; but they will get there too, and sooner rather than later.)

There are more accurate measures of economic activity that are not so warped by 19th century thinking. In the stock market, for example, Google is worth more than GM. However before you can say that growth is good, you need to define what you mean by growth; and make sure that it actually correlates to what you view as an improved world and an increased standard of living. Economic activity for economic activity's sake--what is usually reported on the nightly news when talking heads say things like "The economy grew/shrunk 0.3% in the last quarter" correlates badly if at all with the world I want to live in.
