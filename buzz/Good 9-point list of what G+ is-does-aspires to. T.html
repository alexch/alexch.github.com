About Maps being an acquisition: "Google Maps first started as a C++ program designed by two Danish brothers Lars and Jens Rasmussen at the Sydney-based company Where 2 Technologies. It was first designed to be separately downloaded by users but the company later pitched the idea for a purely Web-based product to Google management, changing the method of distribution." http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google_maps

Sounds like Google basically purchased the maps and the routing technology and ported it to the web. That's entirely different than the way you make it sound, which is buying an existing product and just changing the logo.

In each case, Google acquired the talent and the experience to create a new product. What they got was a starting point and the talent to do the rest. They still provided the vision of where to take the product, how it would work on the web, and how it would interact with their other products.

What Google did was smart, and it's basically what other large corporations do to mitigate risk. They don't start from scratch and they hire the talent that has the experience and know how to follow through the with the grand plan from the executives.

For the same reason, that's why Apple purchased NeXT (which used BSD and a Mach kernel as its base) as a starting point for OS X. That's why Apple used for OS X as a starting point for iOS. That's why Apple purchased FingerWorks for iOS for multitouch functionality. Creating grand new products from scratch rarely happens, for good reason. Jul 16, 2011 Jul 16, 2011

"Plus is me-too Facebook"

That's not a bad thing and not proof that Google can't build a consumer product. Plus is Facebook with more controls around who can see see content.
Facebook is Myspace with real names that was also effectively marketed in a way that is counter-intuitive to a social networking site.
Myspace is Friendster is more personalization.
Friendster built upon the success of SixDegrees...

The point is that every new offering builds upon the successful features of something else and tries to best it in some way, like features, price, or marketing prowess. Yeah, Google's products build on other features in the market place, but they still have to put those features together in a coherent manner and market their product effectively. Sure, other companies might offer products that have more polish due to Google's engineering mindset, but that's hardly proof that Google can't make a consumer products. Consumers still use those products because they make up for it some other way.

"Android is me-too iOS"

I liked the Danger redux spin better. Android was already in heavy development when the iPhone was announced. Copying the best features of your competitor goes both ways. We're better off for it.
