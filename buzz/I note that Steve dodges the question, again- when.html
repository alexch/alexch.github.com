Steve -- I have absolutely no idea what you're talking about.

Chad -- Apple does use the Win32 API, via a portability layer, to run iTunes for Windows.

And Chad, your technical point is identical to mine. It should be a non-issue, but Apple is claiming the right to dictate the "original" language in which you write your code. They've done this to screw with Flash, and other cross-platform toolkits, but have managed to throw out a lot of babies with that bathwater, including programs written in Ruby, Scala, Lua, Scheme, etc., despite the fact that many accepted apps have been written in whole or in part in those languages without any conceivable harm to Apple's brand.

What they're worried about is Flash apps that don't conform to the Apple look and feel, and Flash runtimes that hog memory and burn CPU. Not Ruby apps that directly call the iPhone API and run inside LLVM. It's crazy and stupid and invidious.

See http://jlongster.com/blog/2010/04/09/scheme-dead-iphone/ : "Gambit Scheme compiles to portable C, so there's no problem running it on the iPhone. Many of my blog posts have been discussing the advantages of writing iPhone games in Gambit and how I have solved problems of debugging and rapid development.

Developing Farmageddon in Scheme was an experience I'll never forget. I had access to a Gambit Scheme REPL from my running application, either on the simulator or the device itself. This means that, in combination with Emacs, I was able to develop my game in real-time. Literally. I was able to write code and immediately send it to my running 3d game. It was incredible, and I have a youtube video to prove it.

To top it off, after experiencing such enlightened development practices, I get to publish my game to one of the most popular and accessible markets alive.

But not anymore. Apple made it extremely clear that they don't want any of this going on." May 18, 2010 May 18, 2010
