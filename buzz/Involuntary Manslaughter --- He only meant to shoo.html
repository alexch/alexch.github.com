Been out of the loop for a few days with family in town - but just to add my two cents to this line of thought which has clearly been completed...given the last posting was on July 10th...

I have been the subject to two horrific actions against my personal freedom and property as well, but that is irrelevant to this line of argument. It only calls into suspicion the line of thought of the individual. I only bring it up to say that I understand the consequences of criminal action against individuals. That aside...

The force a criminal uses against their victims is not an issue in this line of thought. They may be dangerous. They may be out of control. And if such actions are excessive, police have weapons at their disposal. As I argued before, the hurdle for the use of force by the police SHOULD be high. Police are the arm of the state that acts to bring citizens to the justice of said state. They ARE in a very difficult position as Michael alluded to. The fact is that no matter what the situation is - the police are dealing with someone who has NEVER been convicted of the crime for which they are being pursued unless the person of interest was convicted in absentia. Under such circumstances, the use of any force in that situation is a non-judicially sanctioned action against that individual. A person who forces the police to resort to such violence do so b/c their actions threaten the police themselves or other citizens and such criminals have forced the issue to extremes.

The individual in this particular case was not innocent of prior actions, but were his actions worthy of a death sentence? By lowering the bar of "police action" the individual was deprived of life by the state simply for being a bit of a dick. That is unacceptable.

On the police side of the issue, the adversarial position of the police with citizens is bad enough as it is. There are arguments that the current police v. citizen relationship is the natural result of Miranda but i really don't care about that one way or another for the purposes of this discussion. Police need to work within the community (much like the lessons learned by the military in Iraq and Afghanistan). Giving the police ANY additional weapons (short of their fists or batons) that they can use against an unarmed citizen is not something I'm comfortable with. The consequences are a police force that has a dangerous weapon (see Kevin's link) that they view as an option in very simple or slightly aggravated situations. Tasers are mocked in movies (see the Hangover...you tube "In the Face!" for an example) and the average person and cop loses respect for the weapon. Say what you will about the NRA but one of their core ideals is that you treat a rifle/pistol with respect as if it could go off at any time. We don't have that with tasers and they can kill people (IBID).

Police are still just humans. They have a difficult and often thankless job to do. The reality is that they often do little to PREVENT crime other then preventing the NEXT crime by catching the individual citizens who would harm others. Tasers minimize and desensitize police to the actions that they are taking. Not all - but enough that it is an issue.
