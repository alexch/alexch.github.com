In his impressive attempt to catalogue these “doses of arsenic,” Klemperer awards pride of place to the words used by the state to describe prisoners, prison camps, and the treatments to which they were subjected. Alex Chaffee 107397735779828096052
Orwell does have a point. Fortunately, there's a countervailing sociolinguistic force at work: over time, the euphemism tends to acquire the same stigma as the original term. My favorite example is the parade of terms used to describe mentally deficient individuals: half-wit, idiot, moron, retarded, challenged, special -- each was adopted in order to remove the stigma associated with the previous term, and each soon reacquired the stigma. I think the same thing has now happened with “enhanced interrogation” -- people pretty much know it means torture.

That doesn't excuse what happened at the time, though, and "pretty much" is very far from perfect, since it still leaves a hole for torture advocates to pretend they're not pro-torture.

BTW thanks for the "ethnic cleansing" example -- it never occurred to me that it would sound positive to some people, but it makes sense that it was a euphemism for genocide. Jul 03, 2010 Jul 03, 2010
